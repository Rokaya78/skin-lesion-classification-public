Week 4 focused on comparing all transfer learning strategies and analyzing the tradeoffs between overall accuracy, balanced multi-class performance, and medically important classification behavior.

1. Baseline CNN (Training from Scratch)
------------------------------------------------
The baseline CNN achieved the weakest performance across nearly all metrics:
- Accuracy: 51.20%
- Macro F1: 0.2655
- AUC: 0.8116

These results demonstrate the difficulty of learning robust medical image features from scratch using a relatively small dataset. Although the baseline model achieved the lowest melanoma-to-bkl dangerous confusion rate (5.9%), overall classification quality and minority-class performance were poor.

The baseline experiment established an important reference point showing that pretrained representations are essential for strong skin lesion classification performance.

2. Feature Extraction (Frozen Backbone)
------------------------------------------------
Feature extraction using a pretrained MobileNetV2 backbone significantly improved performance:
- Accuracy increased to 63.85%
- Macro F1 increased to 0.4639
- AUC increased to 0.8990

This confirms that pretrained ImageNet features transfer effectively to medical imaging tasks.

However, although overall classification improved substantially, melanoma-to-bkl dangerous confusion increased to 19.8%, indicating that the frozen backbone was not sufficiently adapted to distinguish clinically similar lesion categories.

3. Progressive Unfreezing
------------------------------------------------
Progressive unfreezing produced the best overall classification performance:
- Accuracy: 70.60%
- Weighted F1: 0.7317
- Macro F1: 0.6071
- AUC: 0.9450

Gradually unfreezing deeper backbone layers allowed the model to adapt pretrained features to the HAM10000 dataset while preserving training stability.

This strategy achieved the strongest balance between:
- overall accuracy
- generalization
- minority-class recognition
- stable training behavior

The experiment demonstrated that controlled adaptation of pretrained representations is highly effective for medical image classification.

Progressive unfreezing also significantly improved clinically important classes such as melanoma and reduced dangerous melanoma confusion compared to feature extraction.

4. Full Fine-Tuning
------------------------------------------------
Full fine-tuning achieved the highest balanced multi-class performance:
- Macro F1: 0.6519
- AUC: 0.9551
- Recall: 0.7855

Allowing all backbone layers to train enabled deeper specialization to the dataset and produced strong minority-class performance across classes such as:
- akiec
- bcc
- df
- vasc

However, full fine-tuning also showed stronger overfitting behavior:
- training accuracy became much higher than validation accuracy
- overall accuracy decreased compared to progressive unfreezing

Additionally, melanoma performance was slightly weaker than progressive unfreezing, indicating that aggressive adaptation may reduce some clinically important generalization capabilities.

5. Overall Conclusions
------------------------------------------------
The experiments clearly demonstrate the importance of transfer learning for skin lesion classification.

Main findings:
- Training from scratch is insufficient for robust medical image classification.
- Feature extraction provides major performance improvements using pretrained representations.
- Progressive unfreezing offers the best balance between stability and overall classification quality.
- Full fine-tuning achieves the strongest balanced class performance but introduces greater overfitting risk.

From a practical perspective:
- Progressive unfreezing is the best overall classifier.
- Full fine-tuning is the best balanced classifier.

The project successfully demonstrates how different transfer learning strategies affect:
- accuracy
- class balance
- minority-class recognition
- overfitting behavior
- clinically important prediction safety.
